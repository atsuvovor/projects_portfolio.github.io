Process Engineering Case Study

Interactive DMAIC Analysis: Credit Card Sales Process Improvement

Project Overview & Executive Summary

This interactive report details the analysis and improvement plan for critical issues identified within a new credit card sales process at a major financial institution. Implemented six months prior with objectives to enhance speed, ease, and client satisfaction, the process unfortunately led to declining Financial Advisor (FA) adoption, reduced credit card sales, and decreased client satisfaction. This application walks through the DMAIC (Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control) methodology employed to address these challenges.

The goal is to provide a clear understanding of the problem, the data-driven analysis, the proposed solutions, and the expected outcomes, fostering a more efficient, user-friendly, and client-centric sales process. Each section provides insights into how the project progressed, from identifying issues to implementing sustainable solutions.

1. Define the Problem

The Define phase is foundational, establishing the precise problems to be addressed, the overarching project goals, and the key metrics that will be used to track progress and success. For the credit card sales process, it became clear that initial performance post-launch was significantly below expectations, impacting both internal operations and the client experience.

Problem Statement:

The new credit card sales process, implemented six months ago to enhance efficiency and client satisfaction, is failing to meet its objectives. Specifically:

  • Declining Financial Advisor Adoption: The percentage of FAs utilizing the new process steadily decreased from an initial high of 85% to 60% within six months, indicating resistance or difficulty in using the new system.
  • Decreased Credit Card Sales: Overall credit card sales volume suffered a noticeable decline of approximately 33% (from an average of ~180 units/month before to ~120 units/month after) since the new process was introduced.
  • Lower Client Satisfaction: Recent client surveys revealed a drop in average client satisfaction scores from 8.0 (pre-implementation) to 6.5 (post-implementation) on a 1-10 scale, particularly concerning the credit card application experience.

Project Goal:

To identify and address the root causes of declining adoption, sales, and client satisfaction related to the new credit card sales process, ultimately leading to a more efficient, user-friendly, and client-centric process that drives increased sales and satisfaction, aligning with the bank's strategic objectives.

3. Analyze Root Causes

The Analyze phase focuses on dissecting the collected data to identify the underlying reasons—the root causes—for the process deficiencies. Data from Financial Advisor surveys, process cycle time measurements, and sales figures were key to this analysis, helping to pinpoint specific areas of concern that led to the observed negative trends.

Financial Advisor Feedback (Average Ratings)

This horizontal bar chart displays the average ratings (on a 1-5 scale, where 1 is strongly disagree and 5 is strongly agree) from Financial Advisors on various aspects of the new process. Lower scores indicate areas of significant dissatisfaction.

Identified Root Causes:

Based on the quantitative data and qualitative feedback, the following primary root causes for the process's underperformance were identified:

  • C1: Process Complexity/Inefficiency Despite intentions to simplify, the new process was perceived as not easier (avg. rating 2.5) and, critically, had a higher actual average cycle time (approx. 40 mins) compared to the old process (approx. 35 mins). This suggests new steps or system interactions introduced friction.
  • C2: Inadequate Training & Support A very low average rating for "Adequate Training Provided" (1.8) strongly indicated that FAs were not sufficiently prepared or supported to use the new process effectively, leading to errors and frustration.
  • C3: Lack of Perceived Benefit FAs did not strongly believe the new process was faster (avg. rating 3.2) or that it improved client satisfaction (avg. rating 3.0). Without a clear perceived advantage, motivation to adopt waned.
  • C4: Technical Issues/System Usability The average rating for "Technical Issues Experienced" (2.8) pointed to system bugs, slow performance, or a non-intuitive user interface, further hindering adoption and overall efficiency.
  • C5: Resistance to Change / Preference for Old Process A high average rating for "Prefer Old Process" (4.1) signaled natural resistance, likely amplified by the practical difficulties encountered with the new system.

4. Improve the Process

The Improve phase focuses on developing, piloting, and implementing solutions to address the root causes identified in the Analyze phase. The goal is to create a significantly better process that meets the original objectives of efficiency, ease of use, and client satisfaction. The proposed solutions are multi-faceted, targeting process design, user support, technology, and change management.

Proposed Solutions:

1. Process Simplification & Redesign (Addressing C1)
  • Conduct Value Stream Mapping (VSM) workshops with FAs to identify and eliminate non-value-added steps, bottlenecks, and rework loops.
  • Apply Lean principles (e.g., 5S, waste elimination) to streamline workflows, focusing on reducing clicks, minimizing data entry, and simplifying decision points.
  • Develop clear, concise, and visually-aided Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for the optimized process.
2. Enhanced Training & Ongoing Support (Addressing C2)
  • Implement a blended learning approach: interactive online modules, hands-on workshops, and one-on-one coaching.
  • Training to clearly articulate the "why" (benefits of improved process) and "how" (practical, scenario-based guidance).
  • Establish a dedicated, quick-response support channel (e.g., help desk, internal chat group) for immediate queries.
  • Launch a "Super User" program, training experienced FAs as peer-to-peer support and champions.
3. Technology Optimization & Automation (Addressing C1, C4)
  • Conduct a thorough UX/UI review based on FA feedback, prioritizing quick wins for interface improvements.
  • Identify and implement automation for manual steps (e.g., pre-filling client data, automated document generation/routing).
  • Perform a system performance audit to address technical glitches and slow loading times.
4. Communication & Change Management (Addressing C3, C5)
  • Clearly communicate improvements and how they directly address FA pain points and client feedback, emphasizing tangible benefits (e.g., "now 20% faster," "reduced data entry by 3 steps").
  • Ensure strong, visible support and endorsement from senior leadership.
  • Share internal success stories and testimonials from FAs effectively using the improved process.
  • Implement a phased rollout for significant changes, gathering feedback at each stage.

5. Control and Monitor

The Control phase is critical for ensuring that the improvements made to the credit card sales process are sustained over the long term and that the process remains stable and effective. This involves establishing ongoing monitoring mechanisms, standardizing procedures, and creating continuous feedback loops to prevent regression and foster a culture of ongoing improvement.

Control Plan Highlights:

  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs): Formalize and maintain clear, accessible, and up-to-date SOPs for the redesigned process. Ensure all Financial Advisors are trained on and adhere to these.
  • Performance Monitoring Dashboard: Maintain and regularly update the dashboards (similar to those in this application) for key metrics (Adoption Rate, Sales Volume, Client Satisfaction, Process Cycle Time). These will be reviewed weekly/monthly by management and relevant teams.
  • Control Charts: Implement Statistical Process Control (SPC) charts (e.g., X-bar and R charts for cycle time, p-charts for adoption rate) to monitor process stability and detect any out-of-control conditions or significant deviations early, allowing for prompt corrective action.
  • Regular Training Refreshers: Schedule periodic refresher training sessions for FAs, especially for new hires or when minor process updates occur, to reinforce best practices and address any evolving needs.
  • Advisor & Client Feedback Loops: Maintain active and structured channels for FA feedback (e.g., quarterly surveys, dedicated suggestion box, regular team meetings) and client feedback (e.g., post-transaction surveys). Address concerns promptly and incorporate insights into process reviews.
  • Periodic Audits: Conduct periodic internal audits of the credit card sales process to ensure compliance with SOPs, identify any deviations, and verify the effectiveness of implemented controls.
  • Clear Process Ownership: Assign clear ownership for the process to a specific team or individual (e.g., the Process Engineering Manager or a dedicated Process Owner) responsible for its ongoing health, performance, and continuous improvement initiatives.

How We Know We've Solved the Problem (Success Criteria):

Definitive success of the project will be confirmed when the following criteria are consistently met and sustained over a significant period (e.g., 3-6 months post-full implementation of improvements):

  • Financial Advisor Adoption Rate consistently remains above **85-90%**.
  • Monthly Credit Card Sales Volume consistently returns to or exceeds **pre-new process implementation levels (e.g., >180 units/month)**.
  • Average Client Satisfaction Scores for the credit card application process consistently return to or exceed **pre-new process implementation levels (e.g., >8.0 on a 1-10 scale)**.
  • The average Process Cycle Time for credit card sales is consistently **reduced to or below the target (e.g., <30 minutes)**.
  • Consistent positive qualitative feedback from FAs regarding the ease of use, efficiency, and support for the process, and from clients regarding their smooth application experience.
  • Control charts for key metrics demonstrate that the process is in a state of statistical control, indicating stability and predictability with variations only due to common causes.
